It's come to my attention that too many people around me are falling victim to phishing scams, so that's what this spontaneous post is all about.

<!--more-->

I had hoped (most) of this was common knowledge, but apparently not, hope you learn something new and useful.

## tl;dr

- **always** check the link before you open it. Whether that's hovering over it to see a link preview, or just examining it in an SMS.
    - If it's something ending with `.top`, `.info`, `.life` - don't open it. These are cheap top level domains (TLDs) that can be mass bought for phishing purposes.
- take the [Google Phishing Quiz](https://phishingquiz.withgoogle.com/) - quick and helpful for learning how to identify email phishing, but shows how scammers attempt to cleverly obfuscate malicious URLS with legitimate *looking* ones. Don't forget to check that link before opening it.

There are mainly 2 types of scams I see people falling victim to:

- SMS
- Email

## SMS phshing

Usually the primary means of general phishing (as opposed to spearphishing), though this can vary by country. Mass sending of overdue urgent bill notices with a malicious payment link 

## Email phishing

Email phishing, can be hard to spot due to the sheer abundance of it. We all receive multiple emails a day; they are long and mundane. So when the occasional alarming one like `Someone has just accessed your Google account` jumps out, it plays into our *emotional biases*.

## Phone call scams

These are simply annoying. Robot voice press 1 for English 2 for Chinese, just hang up, or better yet press 1 or 2 and waste their time however you like. I haven't heard of anyone as of yet falling for one these, let's not go there. 

## Spearphishing

A sophisticated form of phishing where a malicious actor gathers, or already knows enough information about you to launch a **targeted** attack. Typically with the aim to exploit your emotional biases, these targeted attacks can make you feel vulnerable and in imminent danger, causing you to react faster and fire away your card details into that `paybill-now.top` site (made up of course). Not as common as general mass phishing attempts over SMS, phone call and email.

I once thought I was a spearphishing target because I legitimately had not paid a bill and kept receiving the same SMS about that bill, but turns out it was just a common one. Must be a high success rate?
